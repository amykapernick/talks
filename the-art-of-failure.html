The Art of Failure

We're not strangers to failure, but why does that have to be a bad thing?
Failure is important and it's a chance for us to learn.
To start off, I'm going to take you back to the beginning where this all began
I grew up in a small town in rural Queensland and after high school I moved to Perth to study Engineering
I'd done really well in maths and science throughout high school so Engineering was the obvious choice, what could go wrong?
Engineering Mechanics 100
Engineering Maths 140
Engineering Graphics 232
Strength of Materials 232
Thermodynamics 236
Mechanical Design 238
In Semester 1, I failed Mechanics (did I mention I was studying Mechanical engineering as well?)
In Semester 2, I failed Maths
In Semester 3, I failed Graphics and Strength of Materials
In Semester 4, I failed Thermodynamics and Mechanical Design
Anyone see a trend here?
In Semester 5, I surprisingly didn't fail anything and I learnt something really important

I probably shouldn't be studying Engineering

I failed. And that's ok.

It took me almost 2 and a half years, but I eventually learnt a lesson from my failure
So what am I doing now?
After Engineering I started working fulltime in IT Support at the uni, working in and then managing a team of support officers
During this time I discovered that I loved web devlepment and so started doing freelance web dev on the side
Then 2 years ago I decided to take the leap and started applying to work as a dev full time
This year, I've started rock climbing on a regular basis.
I've found it's a great way to un-wind at the end of the week
It's a great way to exercise
And the feeling when you finally manage to get a tough move or finish a new wall is amazing.
When I started planning this talk, I instantly thought of when I go rock climbing.
When you're climbing, there are often times when you need to jump for a hold.
And sometimes you miss and fall back in the harness.
And that's ok.
It gives you an opportunity to sit back in the harness, and take a look back.
You can learn from it
When I decided I wanted to work in the web fulltime, I did this.
I took a chance
When I decided I wanted to work in the web fulltime, I did this.
I took a chance
Because of that leap, I started working at an agency, and got to work on the web all day, every day
I decided to get more involved in the community and started helping as an organiser for Fenders
The community gave me a chance to learn and I met a whole group of people who did what I do
I love working in web development. I love learning new things, I love discovering new technologies and techniques and in the web industry, there will always be something new
While working at the agency, I continued working as a freelancer on the side as I found it was a great chance to try out new things away from the restrictions of browser support, clients and agency life
This also meant that when I got made redundant a year ago, I had something to fall back on.
So after a small slip, I took another leap and started working for myself full time
Since then I've done contracting work for a bunch of companies across Australia and worked with a wide variety of small to medium businesses.
I also started working for YOW Conferences as an evangelist so get to spend even more time in the community and conferences, meeting others in the tech industry
In the past year, I've got to work with a heap of amazing people on a bunch of cool projects, and learnt a lot of really important skills, both in development and in life
And I've still been able to pay my bills so I'm going to count this as not a failure (yet)

Learn

Learning is important no matter where you are in your career, whether you're a junior, intermediate, senior or even the CTO
This means learning from your mistakes and failures
Although I use git, I use it more for continuous deployment and sharing code when I'm doing subcontracting so I'm not great at my commits and most of them are something like "did stuff" or "it's fixed now"
That was at least until earlier this year when out of the blue my computer died on me. It crashed and then refused to boot and I had to reset the whole thing several times. Of course this was 2 hours before a meeting with a client and I lost everything.
Thankfully the client was also a developer so understood my pain, but I'm much better at pushing my code at regular intervals now, although my git commit messages could still use some work.
But learning is also important to prepare for failure
I take every opportunity I have to learn something new or to try something different
Last year I started learning React in my spare time
This meant that when I had to look for a new job, I'd learnt new skills outside of my existing role
These new skills (although they were very basic) even got me some contracting work when I needed it most, and led to a an ongoing relatinship contracting for a startup
Learning doesn't just mean reading tutorials or doing something constructive.
DO SOMETHING FUN!
When I was first looking for roles in the web, I completed several courses on Codecademy and from one of them I got an idea
I created this game, based on the course and our D&D game, and continued to expand on it
It meant that I had something else to showcase my skills, and the novelty appeared to a lot of people.
One interviewer admitted he'd spent 20 minutes playing it
So do something different, something that interests you
Learning doesn't have to mean doing a side project or using up all your free time either.
Listen to podcasts or read blog posts on your commute, watch conference talks or attend meetups.
Don't feel like you need to spend all your spare time doing countless side projects, we all have lives and people we want to see (well sometimes we want to see them)
There are a bunch of different resources out there, I really enjoy the podcast SyntaxFm, they not only talk about new tech but non technical skills including how to learn

Failure

Failure. Say it to yourself.
The word seems to have so many negative connotations, it just sounds bad.
We need to change the way we look at failure, the way we address it, the way we use the word failure.
And honestly, do we even trust success?
How many times have you run a test or compiled your code, gotten no errors and completely trusted it?

So Failure is Good?

The topic of failure is starting to come up more and more often, and people are starting to see it as a good thing and to talk about it with others
But there are a still a lot of very different attitude and approaches to failure

Fail Fast, Fail Often

With everyone switching to agile, the mantra "Fail fast, fail often" is being used more and more
Recently I saw a TED talk by Leticia Gasca on her experiences around failure and how it led her to start "Fuck up Nights" and the Failure Institute and she uses the mantra

Fail Mindfully

And because I know that anyone who hasn't heard of these before is wondering now...
Fuck up nights is a community with events all over the world where you can get together and share/hear stories of professional fuck ups.
Whether it's a business crashing and burning or a product being recalled, they're helping to remove the stigma behind failing or admitting to failing.
I also like the point they make in "The Subtle Art of Giving a Fuck" which talks about the difference between fault and responsibility
When you've failed, it may not necessarily be your fault, but what you do with it is your responsibility.
Are you going to sit down and blame others for the failure, or are you going to learn from it and take responsibility for what happens next?
When I think of failure, I think of Terry Pratchett's satirical personification of death in the Discworld books
A quirky character and one of my favourite characters, Death helps the characters he encounters come to terms (as they usually aren't thrilled to see him most of the time), has a fondness for cats, a horse called Binky and at one point even calls it quits and becomes a fry cook for a short period.
To me, failure is somewhere between a friend and an acquaintance.
I never make plans to meet up, but when I end up running into them on the street it's nice to catch up

Failure isn't one size fits all

There are so many different types of failures and they all affect everyone differently.
Failure may be
- A mistake at work
- Losing a job
- Personal failures like losing a relationship
- Study relationship (the one that actually comes with an F)
I've experienced all of these failures and they all hit differently
In the same way there's a huge difference between making a mistake at work, and losing a relationship, they won't feel the same
And it's not even different types of failures, there's a huge difference between losing a job you've had for 3 months and a job you've been in for 30 years they'll both affect you differently
When I got made redundant last year, I wasn't in a good place at work and I was actually looking for a different job
I was stressed, I wasn't sleeping, I'd spent the 2 months previous working 60-70 hours each week and it eventually it got to the point where my doctor got me to take a week off work because I was getting dizzy and nauseous.
When I got asked to come into the office even though I was sick, I expected the worst, I thought I was going to get fired, so the redundancy almost came as a relief
But even though it was a good thing, and I'm glad it happened, it still hit me a little (although not as hard as it could have been)

It's ok to not be ok

While it's important to move on from failure, it's also important to let yourself feel it first
When I got made redundant, I called my mum, I cried in my car and then I met a friend for coffee. I let myself feel it.
Then I went home and started applying for jobs.
Failures can be big or small and it's ok to feel it, it's ok to not be ok.
Take a break, take your time, use your support system and don't feel like you have to be fine with it or put on a brave face all the time

You

This is the most important part.
Sometimes you have a really important project that you need to get done, or just want to put another hour in before you leave work
When I was starting out, I found I was more tempted to do this because I wanted to prove that I was a good dev and a hard worker, and I'm still often tempted to do this when I'm working for myself
Make sure you're looking after yourself first, otherwise you risk the failure hitting you harder
I've burnt out before, and I've learnt to recognise the signs and take time out early.
Mental health week was earlier this month and there were events run in the tech community all over Australia. While there's still a way to go, more and more companies and communities are starting to realise the importance and remove the stigma around mental health, burnout and work life balance.

People

People are important.
Community is important.
Over the past year, I've gone through many ups and downs. Some personal, some professional.
But through it all, I had people.
If I had a personal down, I had my friends and family
If I had a professional down, I had Fenders
Melbourne is blessed with a metric fuck-tonne number of community groups and I highly recommend joining one (or all) of them
There's always someone who's been through a similar situation to you, and everyone else just offers support
Make sure you check out some amazing meetups like Junior Dev Melbourne or Google Developer Group. And definitely make sure you check out Fenders if you're ever in Perth
In my last job, I broke the dev server, has anyone else here done that?
Well I broke the dev server on my second day
Needless to say, I was freaking out
It was only day 2 and I'd already taken an entire server offline
But as I found out, we didn't have failure...
We had doughnuts.
If you did something wrong, like:
- not follow a document
- email the wrong client
- not lock the office properly
- break a server
that was a 'doughnutable' offense
This was good because it meant that you weren't afraid of doing the wrong thing and had a chance to learn
There was still a disincentive (have you ever had to buy Krispy Kremes for 11 people?) but you were able to learn and give things a go without fear of losing your job or getting into trouble
Ignoring the fact that we had 3 diabetics in the office, I saw this and thought it was a pretty good approach to 'failures'

Support your Team

Make sure you're supporting your team with their failures
It's easy to start finger pointing, especially when using things like git blame, so make sure you're not starting a witch hunt when people fail at work
Encourage your team so they're not afraid of failing at work, and if everyone's failing occasionally you might even find you've got less finger pointing going on

Embrace Failure

Failure can be a good thing, and it's not something that we should always avoid.
Failure usually means that we've stepped outside of our comfort zones and done something new.
This talk is one of my favourite examples of embracing failure
Last year I gave my first talk at a meetup, they'd themed the night "Failure" and I wrote this talk
While I'd done a lot of public speaking before, it was my first time speaking at a meetup and it was my first time giving a presentation of my own
And today I'm here giving my first conference talk
And as with most new situations, I'm aware that I might fail and embracing it
Thankfully though, I've known that if I fail today I can just use it as a working example of how it's ok to fail pause for laughter
If we don't open ourselves up to failure, we miss the opportunity to try something new
Failure is a fact of life and very important for learning
There is nothing wrong with not getting it right the first time
Take the time to sit back and learn from the experience
And don't be afraid to jump for it again (but you know, if you broke dev, maybe don't do the exact same thing again)
And even more importantly, take the time away from work
Spend time in the community and with friends
And look after yourself, don't be afraid to ask for help or take a break when you need it
And don't be afraid of failure, because the only way you can truly fail is if you fail to try
