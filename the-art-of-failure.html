Failure

Failure. Say it to yourself. The word seems to have so many negative connotations, it just sounds bad. We need to change the way we look at failure, the way we address it, the way we use the word failure. And honestly, do we even trust success? How many times have you run a test or compiled your code, gotten no errors and completely trusted it? Over the weekend I migrated a website from my old hosting to my new one. Nothing too fancy, just a simple Wordpress build but when I finished the migration and the website worked I was still skeptical. Experience has taught me that sometimes you just can't trust it when things seem to work. As it was, something had gone horribly wrong (which may have also been related to the fact that I upgraded my php version to 7.2 around the same time), and the admin console was completely broken. My simple Wordpress migration ended up taking me a week, and the site was actually offline for a few hours, and had a temporary landing page for 2 days. To start, I'm going to give you a couple of examples of my professional experiences of failure
Frustrated computer user
In my last job, I broke the dev server, has anyone else here done that? Well I broke the dev server on my second day Needless to say, I was freaking out It was only day 2 and I'd already taken an entire server offline But as I found out, we didn't have failure...
Doughnuts
We had doughnuts. If you did something wrong, like: - not follow a document - email the wrong client - not lock the office properly - break a server that was a 'doughnutable' offense This was good because it meant that you weren't afraid of doing the wrong thing and had a chance to learn There was still a disincentive (have you ever had to buy Krispy Kremes for 11 people?) but you were able to learn and give things a go without fear of losing your job or getting into trouble Ignoring the fact that we had 3 diabetics in the office, I saw this and thought it was a pretty good approach to 'failures'
Phones
When I worked at Curtin, we received funding to run a 24 hour student helpdesk. This was an idea that continued to get floated, and we already knew that we didn't need this, but another department was funding it and insisted we give it a go. 4 weeks later, we'd received 2 phone calls to the helpdesk. I guess you could call it a failure But it was a 'good failure'. Now we had physical proof that a 24 hour helpdesk wasn't worth it. We learnt from it

Embrace Failure

Failure can be a good thing, and it's not something that we should always avoid. Failure usually means that we've stepped outside of our comfort zones and done something new. Tonight I'm embracing failure in many ways. While I've done a lot of public speaking before, tonight is my first time speaking at a meetup. And as with most new situations, I'm aware that I might fail and embracing it Thankfully though, I've known that even if I failed tonight, I'd just use it as a working example *pause for laughter* I even had the chance to learn how to animate for my rock climbing animation before (although as you could tell, I didn't get the chance to learn to draw) If we don't open ourselves up to failure, we miss the opportunity to try something new
While we can change the way we look at failure, it often still hurts when it happens. A month ago, I got made redundant. It's hard to not think of that as a failure, and there are a lot of things that help make it easier.

People

People are important. Community is important. Over the past year, I've gone through many ups and downs. Some personal, some professional. But through it all, I had people. If I had a personal down, I had my friends and family If I had a professional down, I had Fenders Perth is blessed with a metric fuck-tonne number of community groups and I highly recommend joining one (or all) of them There's always someone who's been through a similar situation to you, and everyone else just offers support

Learn

This is something that's always important and I believe especially so when you're a junior dev This means learning from your mistakes and failures But this is also important to prepare for failure I take every opportunity I have to learn something new or to try something different I'd even been learning React in my spare time (there's not much of it) This meant that when I was looking for a new job, I'd learnt new skills outside of my existing role
D&D Game
Learning doesn't just mean reading tutorials or doing something constructive. DO SOMETHING FUN! When I was first looking for roles in the web, I completed several courses on Codecademy and from one of them I got an idea I created this game, based on the course and our D&D game, and continued to expand on it It meant that I had something else to showcase my skills, and the novelty appeared to a lot of people. One interviewer admitted he'd spent 20 minutes playing it So do something different, something that interests you

You

This is the most important part. Apologies for going a little off-topic, but this is really important Sometimes you have a really important project that you need to get done, or just want to put another hour in before you leave work As a junior dev, I found I was more tempted to do this because I wanted to prove that I was a good dev and a hard worker Make sure you're looking after yourself first, otherwise you risk the failure hitting you harder I've burnt out before, and I've learnt to recognise the signs and take time out early. (Shamless self promotion) Fenders is trying to raise awareness and acceptance of mental health importance nad burnout and ran several events for Geek Mental Help Week in October. Keep an eye out for more events
Failure is a fact of life and very important for learning There is nothing wrong with not getting it right the first time Take the time to sit back and learn from the experience And don't be afraid to jump for it again (but you know, if you broke dev, maybe don't do the exact same thing again) And even more importantly, take the time away from work Spend time in the community and with friends And look after yourself, don't be afraid to ask for help or take a break when you need it But don't be afraid of failure.
